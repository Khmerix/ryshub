A1: Stress on Content Words versus Function Words
Content vs. Function Words

Content words carry meaning and are usually stressed (spoken more clearly and loudly): nouns, pronouns, main verbs, adjectives, adverbs.

Function words do not carry meaning and are usually unstressed (spoken more quickly): articles, auxiliary verbs, conjunctions, determiners, prepositions.

A3: Reducing to Schwa: "to"
🔊 Reducing to Schwa: "to" - Audio
Schwa Reduction

Reduction occurs in spoken language when sounds are shortened. In English, the vowel sound in the word "to" is often reduced from /tuː/ to schwa /tə/.

Examples:
• Elias covered his head with a towel to /tə/ keep cool.
• My class ends at a quarter to /tə/ two.
• I need to /tə/ speak to him in the morning.

A4: Intonation to Show Sarcasm and Irony
🔊 Intonation for Sarcasm and Irony - Audio
Sarcasm is the use of irony, the opposite of what is actually meant, to be humorous or maybe even insulting. In spoken English, sarcasm is largely expressed by the way the words in a statement or question are said.

A5: Reducing to Schwa: High-Frequency Function Words
🔊 High-Frequency Function Words - Audio
Function words carry very little meaning but have a grammatical role. The vowel sounds in function words are frequently reduced to schwa /ə/ in English.

Examples:
Moira can /kən/ help you with that.
The only model we have left is orange and /ənd/ green.
The dogs will /wəl/ run away if you leave the door open.

A6: The Prominence of Stress
🔊 The Prominence of Stress - Audio
Stress Changes Meaning

In speech, you can stress different words to modify the meaning. The same sentence said in different ways can have slightly different meanings.

Example: Artists often make sacrifices for their work.
• Artists often make... (One type of person)
• Artists often make... (They do it all the time, not occasionally)
• Artists often make sacrifices... (Their commitment takes personal sacrifice)

A Practice: Identifying Emphasized Meaning

Listen to the sentences and circle the stressed word in each sentence. Then choose the emphasized meaning.

a. Sherry's action was "took."
b. Sherry took the picture, and not someone else.
c. Sherry took a picture, and not something else.

A7: Intonation in Tag Questions
🔊 Intonation in Tag Questions - Audio
Rising Intonation: Indicates the speaker thinks the statement is true but isn't certain. The speaker is asking a real question.

Falling Intonation: Indicates stronger certainty. The speaker is not asking a true question so much as anticipating agreement.

Grammar Points from Units 7-12

Unit 7: Noun Clauses with Wh- Words and If/Whether
Forming Noun Clauses
Type
Rule
Example
wh- clauses
Combine a wh- question and a statement. Change the question to statement word order.
She taught sixty students. How does she do it?
→ I don't know how she teaches sixty students.
if/whether clauses
Combine a yes/no question and a statement.
Did he pass the exam? I wonder.
→ I wonder if/whether he passed the exam (or not).
Important Note: If and whether are often interchangeable, but they are not quite the same.
• Use if when the noun clause outlines one condition.
• Use whether (or not) when the noun clause states alternative possibilities.

Example:
Let Anne know if Jack is coming. (Anne only needs to be contacted if Jack is coming.)
Let Anne know whether Jack is coming. (Anne needs to be contacted whether he comes or not.)
A Common Expressions

Many of these expressions are used in the negative when forming wh- or if/whether clauses:

not certain
don't see (= don't understand)
forget*
not sure
have no idea
can't tell (= can't figure it out)
don't know
don't understand
can't remember
wonder*

*Do not use the negative with these verbs.

Unit 8: Reported Questions
Quoted Speech vs. Reported Speech
Quoted Speech
Reported Speech
Yes/no questions:
Are you going to win an award?
They asked him if he was going to win an award.
They asked him whether (or not) he was going to win an award.
Wh- questions:
Who are you here with?
He asked him who he was there with.
They wanted to know where he was going.
Key Changes in Reported Questions
  • Notice how the verb forms change (backshift) in reported questions.
  • Notice the shift from here to there.
  • You can use the expression want to know in place of ask.
Unit 9: Stating Conditions - Other Ways of Expressing If
Alternative Conditional Expressions
Expression
Example
Meaning
only after / only if
I can take a break (but) only after I study for an hour.
I can take a break if (and only if) I study for an hour.
provided that / as long as
I can take a break provided that I study for an hour.
= If I study for an hour, I can take a break.
unless
I'll be sleepy unless I have a cup of coffee first.
= I'll be sleepy if I don't have a cup of coffee first.
otherwise
You should set your alarm. Otherwise, you won't wake up.
= If you don't (set your alarm), you won't wake up.
even if
I wouldn't join that gym, even if you gave me a free membership.
Nothing can make me join that gym.
Inversion with Only If:
Only if I study for an hour can I take a break. (formal)
Only if we locate more medicinal plants can we cure the patients.
Unit 10: Articles - A/An, The, and No Article (Ø)
Article Usage Rules
Article
Use
Example
a/an
For count nouns in general or when mentioning something for the first time
Do you have a pen I can borrow? (Any pen will do.)
the
For specific nouns the speaker and listener already know about
Can you turn off the faucet? The water is running.
Ø (no article)
To talk about people or things in general
In parts of Africa, Ø water is scarce.
B Expressions That Use (or Don't Use) Articles

Expressions That Use THE

  • Common experiences: taking the bus, reading the newspaper
  • Oceans, rivers: the Indian Ocean, the Nile
  • Mountain ranges: the Urals, the Seychelles
  • Ordinals: the second person, the longest river
  • Hotels, theaters: the Oriental Bangkok

Expressions That Don't Need Articles

  • Certain places: in prison, at work, at home
  • Certain pairs: day after day, husband and wife
  • Fixed expressions: by car, on foot, at night
  • Continents, countries: Africa, Jamaica (exceptions: the Netherlands)
  • Languages: I speak Italian
Unit 11: Adjective Clauses with Object Relative Pronouns
Relative Pronouns
Clause Type
Describing People
Describing Things
Restrictive clauses
(necessary information)
The most creative person who(m)/that/Ø I know is my mother.
The computer which/that/Ø I use was obviously designed by a creative thinker.
Nonrestrictive clauses
(extra information)
My best friend, who(m) I've known since childhood, is very creative.
This computer, which I bought last week, was obviously designed by a creative thinker.
Important Rules:
• In restrictive clauses, you can omit the relative pronoun when it is the object.
• In nonrestrictive clauses, you cannot omit the relative pronoun.
• Nonrestrictive clauses are separated from the main clause by commas.
• While whom is grammatically correct, in spoken English you will most often hear who in its place.

Intonation in Tag Questions

Falling Intonation (↘): Use when you are sure of the answer and expect agreement.
Example: That's hilarious, ↘isn't it? (I'm sure it is funny)

Rising Intonation (↗): Use when you are unsure and want to check information.
Example: You're coming, ↗aren't you? (I'm not sure - checking)
